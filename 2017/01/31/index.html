Tuesday, January 31, 2017

Donald J. Trump's Day: Supreme Court Nomination

A chronological account of executive actions, public appearances, and notable developments
Advertisement

WASHINGTON - President Trump nominated Judge Neil M. Gorsuch of the 10th Circuit Court of Appeals to the Supreme Court in a prime-time East Room ceremony, fulfilling his campaign promise to replace Justice Antonin Scalia with a conservative jurist (Roll Call).

Daily Chronology

Day: Trump finalizes Supreme Court selection process, with Gorsuch emerging as the choice from a list of potential nominees (ABC News).
Afternoon: White House prepares for prime-time announcement ceremony in the East Room, with staff coordinating media coverage (Roll Call).
Evening: Trump delivers remarks introducing Gorsuch, praising his "extraordinary résumé" and "qualifications beyond dispute" (Roll Call).
Prime time: Gorsuch accepts the nomination, noting he clerked for Justice Byron White, "the last justice from Colorado and the only justice to lead the NFL in rushing" (Roll Call).

Executive Actions

  • Nominated Judge Neil M. Gorsuch to the Supreme Court to fill the seat left vacant by Justice Antonin Scalia's death (White House Archives).
  • Called on Congress to confirm Gorsuch, who was previously confirmed to the 10th Circuit by voice vote in 2006 (Roll Call).

Press Coverage

  • Roll Call: Focused on the confirmation battle ahead and Senate Democrats' plans to require a 60-vote threshold for approval (Read more)
  • ABC News: Highlighted Gorsuch's credentials as the youngest nominee since Clarence Thomas and his originalist judicial philosophy
  • The Washington Post: Analyzed Gorsuch as a "less bombastic version of Scalia" with strong originalist interpretation credentials
  • Alliance for Justice: Called the nomination "disastrous," expressing concern about corporate favoritism in his judicial record

Historical Significance

January 31, 2017, marked Trump's first Supreme Court nomination, selecting a 49-year-old judge who would be the youngest justice since Clarence Thomas. Gorsuch's nomination came after Senate Republicans had blocked President Obama's nominee Merrick Garland for over 300 days, arguing the next president should fill Scalia's seat. The selection fulfilled Trump's campaign promise to conservative voters who viewed the Supreme Court as a crucial election issue (Roll Call).

The nomination set up an immediate confirmation battle, with Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer announcing Democrats would require a 60-vote threshold typically associated with a filibuster. Gorsuch's sterling academic credentials—including clerking for two Supreme Court justices and his unanimous 2006 Senate confirmation—complicated Democratic opposition strategies. If confirmed, he would maintain the Court's conservative-liberal balance that existed before Scalia's death, with his originalist judicial philosophy closely mirroring that of his predecessor (ABC News).
